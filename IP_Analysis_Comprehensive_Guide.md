## 1. Landscape Analysis

**Purpose:** Map the full breadth of patent activity in a technology domain to understand who owns what, where activity is concentrated, and how the field has evolved.

### Phase 1: Scope Definition

1. Define the primary technology domain with specific technical boundaries (e.g., "solid-state lithium-ion batteries" vs. "batteries").
2. Identify sub-domains, adjacent domains, and enabling technologies that should be included.
3. Define geographic scope: global, regional (e.g., US + EP + CN + JP + KR), or jurisdiction-specific.
4. Define temporal scope: full history vs. rolling window (e.g., past 10–20 years).
5. Identify key technical vocabulary: preferred terms, synonyms, abbreviations, trade names, chemical names, IPC/CPC classification codes.
6. Document all scope decisions and rationale for reproducibility.

### Phase 2: Search Strategy Development

7. Construct keyword search strings using Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT) for each sub-domain.
8. Map relevant IPC (International Patent Classification) codes for the domain.
9. Map relevant CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification) codes for granular sub-domains.
10. Identify relevant US Class/Subclass codes for US-specific searches.
11. Develop assignee/applicant name lists for targeted entity-based searches.
12. Build inventor name lists for individual-level tracking (key inventors in the field).
13. Plan citation-based search strategies (forward/backward citation chaining) for completeness.
14. Document all queries with version control for repeatability.

### Phase 3: Data Collection

15. Execute searches across primary databases: USPTO, EPO (Espacenet), WIPO (PatentScope), JPO, CNIPA (China), KIPO (South Korea).
16. Execute searches across commercial databases as available: Derwent Innovation, Orbit Intelligence, PatSnap, LexisNexis TotalPatent, CPA Global.
17. Retrieve all relevant patent families (not just individual patent documents) to avoid double-counting.
18. Download full bibliographic data: title, abstract, claims, assignee, inventor, filing date, publication date, priority date, legal status, family members.
19. Collect citation data: forward citations (who cites this patent) and backward citations (what this patent cites).
20. Collect legal status data: granted, pending, abandoned, expired, lapsed, licensed, litigated.
21. Remove duplicates, non-relevant results, and noise records using systematic deduplication.
22. Record total raw counts and post-deduplication counts.

### Phase 4: Data Normalization & Enrichment

23. Standardize assignee names (handle subsidiaries, merged entities, name variants, spelling variations, translations).
24. Map assignee entities to parent companies using corporate hierarchy databases.
25. Classify patents into sub-domain buckets using CPC codes and manual review sampling.
26. Tag patent families by technology function (component, method, system, material, application).
27. Resolve inventor disambiguation where needed.
28. Enrich records with country-of-origin for inventors and assignees.

### Phase 5: Quantitative Analysis

29. Calculate total patent counts by year (filing date and publication date) to identify activity trends.
30. Calculate patent counts by assignee — identify top 10, 20, 50 players.
31. Calculate patent counts by geography (country of filing, country of assignee).
32. Calculate patent counts by IPC/CPC sub-category to identify technical concentration.
33. Compute family size distributions (number of jurisdictions per family) as a proxy for perceived value.
34. Calculate citation metrics: average forward citations, highly-cited patents (top 10%), h-index by assignee.
35. Compute patent activity indices: compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of filings by period.
36. Identify patent filing peaks — correlate with product launches, standards events, or research breakthroughs.
37. Analyze time-to-grant distributions across jurisdictions.
38. Calculate abandonment and lapse rates by assignee and technology sub-area.

### Phase 6: Qualitative Analysis

39. Review representative patents in each sub-domain cluster — identify claim scope and dominant claim types (apparatus, method, composition, system).
40. Identify blocking patents: broad, foundational claims in core technical areas.
41. Identify improvement patents and design-arounds.
42. Identify standard-essential patents (SEPs) or declared FRAND patents in relevant standards bodies.
43. Identify patent thickets: dense overlapping claim coverage in narrow technical areas.
44. Trace technology lineage through citation chains — identify foundational vs. derivative IP.
45. Identify collaborative filings (co-assignees) to reveal R&D alliances.

### Phase 7: Visualization & Reporting

46. Create filing trend chart: patents filed per year by top assignees (stacked area or multi-line).
47. Create geographic heat map: patent filing density by country.
48. Create assignee ranking table: total families, active families, citation counts.
49. Create technology distribution chart: patent counts by IPC/CPC sub-category (pie, treemap, or sunburst).
50. Create assignee-technology matrix: heatmap showing which companies are active in which sub-domains.
51. Create citation network diagram: show key influencer patents and citation clusters.
52. Create patent family age profile: proportion of portfolio by remaining life.
53. Compile executive summary: key findings, dominant players, technology hotspots, filing trends.
54. Document methodology, search queries, databases used, and retrieval dates for reproducibility.

## 2. Competitive Intelligence

**Purpose:** Understand competitor IP strategies, portfolio strengths and weaknesses, R&D directions, and business intentions derived from patent data.

### Phase 1: Competitor Identification & Profiling

1. Define the competitive set: direct competitors, indirect competitors, new entrants, and potential disruptors.
2. Identify all legal entities associated with each competitor: subsidiaries, affiliates, joint ventures, special-purpose entities.
3. Retrieve all historical name variants and M&A history for each entity.
4. Compile initial profile: headquarters, major products/services, revenue (if public), R&D spending, known technology focus areas.
5. Identify key technical personnel (CTO, lead inventors) for each competitor.

### Phase 2: Portfolio Data Collection

6. Execute assignee-based patent searches for each entity and all affiliated entities across all major jurisdictions.
7. Aggregate results into patent families per competitor.
8. Collect filing dates, priority dates, publication dates, and grant dates for temporal analysis.
9. Collect legal status for all families: granted, pending, abandoned, expired.
10. Collect prosecution history summaries where available (especially for granted US patents via USPTO PAIR/Patent Center).
11. Track pending applications — these reveal future IP intentions before products launch.

### Phase 3: Portfolio Structure Analysis

12. Segment each competitor's portfolio by technology domain and sub-domain.
13. Identify core vs. peripheral technologies for each competitor.
14. Calculate portfolio size metrics: total families, active families, pending families.
15. Calculate portfolio age and remaining average life (weighted by family size).
16. Identify the proportion of portfolio expiring in 0–5, 5–10, and 10+ year windows.
17. Calculate citation impact metrics per competitor: average forward citations, citation growth rate.
18. Identify each competitor's most-cited "crown jewel" patents (top 5–10 by forward citations).
19. Identify each competitor's key inventors and track inventor mobility (attrition to competitors or startups).

### Phase 4: Strategic R&D Direction Analysis

20. Analyze filing trends by sub-domain per competitor over time — identify acceleration or deceleration in specific areas.
21. Identify new technology categories where a competitor filed patents in the last 1–3 years (indicates emerging R&D focus).
22. Identify technology categories where a competitor has stopped filing (indicates de-prioritization or decision to maintain as trade secret).
23. Compare filing dates vs. product launch dates to estimate competitor R&D-to-market timelines.
24. Identify patterns of continuation and divisional filings — indicates active prosecution strategy and claim broadening.
25. Identify CIP (continuation-in-part) filings — indicates incremental additions to existing technology bases.
26. Analyze the ratio of method vs. apparatus vs. composition claims — reveals commercialization strategy (licensing vs. product exclusivity).

### Phase 5: Geographic & Filing Strategy Analysis

27. Map competitor patent filing geography — identify which markets they protect vs. ignore.
28. Identify jurisdictions where a competitor files broadly (strategic markets) vs. selectively (cost control).
29. Identify PCT filings and national phase entry patterns — reveals commercialization geography.
30. Identify design patent and utility model filings by competitor (important in CN, DE, JP, KR).
31. Identify trade secret signals: technology areas with R&D spend but no patent filings.

### Phase 6: Relationship & Collaboration Mapping

32. Identify co-assignments: patents filed jointly with universities, research institutes, or other companies — reveals R&D partners.
33. Identify license agreements from SEC filings, annual reports, press releases, and litigation records.
34. Map technology transfer agreements (especially from academic institutions).
35. Identify participation in standards bodies and declared SEPs.
36. Identify patent pools that competitors participate in.

### Phase 7: Offensive & Defensive Strategy Assessment

37. Identify blocking patents: broad claims covering competitor's core products or methods.
38. Identify competitor patents that may read on your own products or pipeline (freedom-to-operate risk).
39. Identify patents that have been asserted in litigation or licensing demands.
40. Identify patents that have been licensed to third parties.
41. Assess assertion risk: analyze whether competitor has history of offensive licensing, NPE partnerships, or direct litigation.

### Phase 8: Synthesis & Competitive Benchmarking

42. Create comparative portfolio scorecard: size, quality, breadth, depth, geographic reach for each competitor.
43. Create competitor IP strategy profiles: are they offensive, defensive, licensing-focused, or trade-secret-focused?
44. Identify gaps in competitors' portfolios — areas where they have weak or no IP coverage.
45. Identify areas of competitor strength that pose filing or commercialization risk to your organization.
46. Produce competitive intelligence report with visual dashboards and strategic recommendations.
47. Establish monitoring cadence: set up automated alerts for new filings by all tracked entities.

## 3. Freedom to Operate (FTO)

**Purpose:** Determine whether a specific product, process, or service can be commercialized in target markets without infringing unexpired third-party patent claims.

### Phase 1: Product/Process Definition

1. Obtain a complete technical description of the product, process, or service — include all components, sub-components, materials, steps, parameters, and configurations.
2. Define all intended modes of use, configurations, and variants (including optional features that will be offered).
3. Identify target markets (countries/jurisdictions) where commercialization is planned.
4. Identify planned manufacturing locations, supply chain countries, and distribution channels — FTO applies in each jurisdiction separately.
5. Identify the expected commercialization timeline (launch date) to define relevant patent expiry window.
6. Obtain any existing IP clearance opinions, prior FTO analyses, or legal memos related to the product.

### Phase 2: Technical Feature Decomposition

7. Break down the product/process into discrete technical features or "claim elements."
8. For each feature, document the technical implementation in detail (materials, dimensions, methods, parameters).
9. Create a feature matrix: a structured table listing every technical feature by category (structural, functional, method, composition).
10. Identify all technical variations and equivalents that could be considered for each feature.
11. Identify any features that are novel vs. features that are standard/prior art (to prioritize clearance effort).
12. Consult with technical subject matter experts (engineers, scientists) to ensure completeness.

### Phase 3: Search Strategy Development

13. Generate keyword search terms for each technical feature — use preferred terms, synonyms, alternative nomenclature.
14. Map IPC and CPC classification codes relevant to each technical feature.
15. Identify key assignees active in the relevant technology space (from landscape or competitive intelligence).
16. Plan citation-based searches to find related patents not captured by keywords alone.
17. Define date filter: only unexpired patents are relevant — calculate expiry windows based on priority date + 20 years (utility) and jurisdiction-specific adjustments (patent term extensions, PTA adjustments in US).
18. Define geographic scope: run separate searches per target jurisdiction.

### Phase 4: Patent Retrieval & Initial Filtering

19. Execute keyword + classification searches in each target jurisdiction's database.
20. Execute assignee-targeted searches for known active players.
21. Execute citation-based forward searches from foundational patents identified in landscape analysis.
22. Collect all candidate patents and applications.
23. Apply initial filter: remove patents expired before planned launch date in each jurisdiction.
24. Apply initial filter: remove patents clearly unrelated based on title/abstract review.
25. Document retrieval counts at each stage.

### Phase 5: Independent Claim Analysis

26. For each candidate patent, retrieve all independent claims in full text.
27. Parse each independent claim into individual claim elements (limitations).
28. Create a claim chart: map each claim element to the corresponding feature of the product/process.
29. Apply the "all elements rule": a claim is infringed only if every element of the claim is present in the accused product — document analysis for each element.
30. For each element, determine: present, absent, or potentially present (equivalent).
31. Apply the doctrine of equivalents (DOE) for elements not literally present: assess whether the product performs the same function, in substantially the same way, to achieve substantially the same result.
32. Identify "means-plus-function" claims (35 USC § 112(f)) and analyze scope under disclosed structure + equivalents.
33. Consider prosecution history estoppel: check prosecution history for arguments that may limit claim scope (reduces DOE risk).
34. Document the conclusion for each claim: "No concern," "Potential concern," or "High concern."

### Phase 6: Dependent Claim Review (as needed)

35. For patents where at least one independent claim raises concern, review dependent claims — they may add limitations that further narrow or eliminate risk.
36. Document analysis for any dependent claims that raise additional specific concerns.

### Phase 7: Legal Status Verification

37. Verify current legal status for all "concern" patents in each target jurisdiction: active, expired, abandoned, lapsed, or subject to reexamination/IPR/opposition.
38. Check for inter partes review (IPR) or post-grant review (PGR) proceedings — pending invalidation may reduce risk.
39. Check for any licensing declarations (FRAND, RAND) for SEP candidates.
40. Verify patent term expiry accounting for: 20-year term, PTAs (US), SPCs (EU), pediatric exclusivity, and any terminal disclaimers.
41. Check for any assignment records — ensure the identified owner is the current owner.

### Phase 8: Risk Stratification & Clearance Path Development

42. Classify each patent into risk tiers: Tier 1 (high — likely infringement, owner is active/assertive), Tier 2 (moderate — possible infringement, uncertain), Tier 3 (low — unlikely infringement or low assertion risk), Tier 4 (no concern).
43. For Tier 1 and 2 patents, develop clearance paths:
    - **Design-around:** Modify product to remove or alter the infringing element.
    - **License negotiation:** Seek a license from the patent owner.
    - **Challenge validity:** File IPR, ex parte reexamination, or opposition to invalidate the claim.
    - **Non-infringing alternative:** Identify an alternative implementation.
    - **Opinion of counsel:** Obtain a written opinion to support good-faith defense against willful infringement claims.
44. Assess each clearance path for technical feasibility, commercial impact, cost, and timeline.

### Phase 9: Opinion & Reporting

45. Prepare a written FTO opinion or summary memorandum:
    - Executive summary with overall risk rating.
    - Methodology and scope limitations.
    - Claim charts for all Tier 1/2 patents.
    - Recommended clearance paths.
    - Recommended monitoring list (pending applications).
46. Flag any patent applications (not yet granted) that could mature into blocking positions — add to monitoring list.
47. Define re-analysis triggers: product feature changes, new patent grants, M&A activity by key patent owners.
48. Retain qualified patent counsel for formal legal opinion where commercialization decisions are material.

## 4. White Space Analysis

**Purpose:** Identify technology areas, claim types, or combinations of features where patent coverage is absent or sparse — revealing innovation opportunities, filing opportunities, or market entry opportunities.

### Phase 1: Domain Mapping & Landscape Foundation

1. Conduct (or leverage) a Landscape Analysis to obtain a comprehensive view of the patent space.
2. Define the technology domain with multi-dimensional axes: functional area, application domain, material/composition, manufacturing method, end-use market.
3. Define the granularity of analysis: high-level sub-domains vs. specific claim element combinations.
4. Identify all known technical solutions, known prior art, and commercial products in the domain.

### Phase 2: Framework Construction

5. Build a technology matrix with at least two dimensions (e.g., function × application; material × method; component × performance parameter).
6. Populate each cell in the matrix with patent counts from the landscape search.
7. Identify cells with zero or low patent counts — initial white space candidates.
8. Build a claim element co-occurrence matrix: which pairs or triplets of technical features appear together in claims vs. which combinations are unclaimed.
9. Identify functional claim white spaces: functions that are achieved commercially but for which no method/apparatus patents exist.
10. Identify application-specific white spaces: technology is patented for one application but not adapted/claimed for adjacent applications.

### Phase 3: Prior Art Analysis for White Space Validation

11. For each candidate white space, conduct targeted prior art searches to confirm absence of patent coverage (not just absence in initial search).
12. Search non-patent literature (NPL): academic papers, technical standards, conference proceedings, product manuals — to identify whether the white space is truly uncharted or merely unpatented.
13. Distinguish between:
    - **True white space:** No patents, no NPL — genuine invention opportunity.
    - **Prior-art white space:** No patents, but NPL exists — patenting is blocked by prior art, but freedom to operate may exist.
    - **Apparent white space:** Covered by broad claims in existing patents even without specific disclosure.
14. Identify patents with broad claims that may already cover the white space through literal scope or doctrine of equivalents.
15. Document evidence of patentability (novelty + non-obviousness) for each validated white space.

### Phase 4: Commercial & Technical Feasibility Assessment

16. Evaluate each white space for technical feasibility: is it achievable with known science and engineering?
17. Evaluate each white space for commercial relevance: does it address an unmet market need or improve on current solutions?
18. Evaluate the development pathway: time to prototype, estimated R&D cost, regulatory requirements.
19. Identify whether competitors are actively working toward the white space (based on pending applications or published research).
20. Prioritize white spaces using a scoring framework: Technical Feasibility × Commercial Value × Competitive Urgency × Patentability.

### Phase 5: Filing Opportunity Assessment

21. Identify white spaces suitable for new patent filings: novel and non-obvious over prior art.
22. Identify white spaces suitable for defensive publication: to prevent competitors from patenting without your organization needing the exclusive position.
23. Identify white spaces suitable for trade secret protection: where disclosure through patent filing would harm competitive position.
24. Assess claim strategy for each patentable white space: broad method claims, composition claims, apparatus claims, or use claims.

### Phase 6: Strategic Opportunity Mapping

25. Map white spaces to business priorities: which white spaces align with planned products, services, or licensing strategy?
26. Identify freedom-to-operate benefits of white spaces: areas where commercialization requires no licensing.
27. Identify white spaces that, if patented, could create blocking positions against competitors.
28. Identify white spaces in expiring patent territories: patents expiring in 1–5 years that will open up currently restricted areas.
29. Identify geographic white spaces: technology patented in major markets but not in emerging markets (opportunity for low-cost filing).

### Phase 7: Reporting & Action Planning

30. Create a visual white space map: technology matrix with heat-coded cells (red = dense, green = sparse).
31. Rank white space opportunities by strategic priority.
32. Produce action recommendations: file, monitor, design-around, invest in R&D, or defensively publish.
33. Define timelines and owners for each recommended action.
34. Schedule periodic refresh of white space analysis (every 6–12 months as filings evolve).

## 5. Portfolio Assessment

**Purpose:** Evaluate the overall quality, strategic alignment, coverage, and business value of an existing patent portfolio to inform filing, maintenance, pruning, and licensing decisions.

### Phase 1: Portfolio Data Compilation

1. Compile the full patent portfolio from internal records (docket management system).
2. Cross-reference against official patent office records to confirm accuracy of ownership, legal status, and maintenance fee status.
3. Reconcile discrepancies between internal records and official records.
4. Classify each asset: utility patent, design patent, plant patent, utility model, patent application (pending), provisional application.
5. Record key dates for each asset: priority date, filing date, publication date, grant date, calculated expiry date (accounting for PTA, SPC, terminal disclaimers).
6. Record maintenance fee schedule and next due date for each jurisdiction.

### Phase 2: Legal Quality Assessment

7. Review claims of each patent for scope and clarity:
    - Identify independent claims and count claim elements (fewer elements = broader claim).
    - Identify claim types: apparatus, method, composition, system.
    - Identify presence of functional claiming, means-plus-function, or open-ended claim language.
8. Review prosecution history for each patent: identify claim amendments made to overcome prior art (narrowing amendments reduce scope).
9. Assess claim validity risk:
    - Search for prior art that may anticipate or render obvious the key claims.
    - Review any post-grant challenges (IPR, PGR, ex parte reexamination, oppositions).
    - Identify double-patenting issues resolved by terminal disclaimers.
10. Rate each patent on claim quality: Tier A (broad, likely valid), Tier B (moderate scope, moderate validity risk), Tier C (narrow or questionable validity), Tier D (likely invalid or commercially irrelevant).

### Phase 3: Technical Coverage Assessment

11. Map each patent to specific technical domains, sub-domains, and features using a consistent taxonomy.
12. Identify whether each patent is foundational (covers core technology) or improvement/peripheral.
13. Identify whether each patent covers a current product, pipeline product, or neither.
14. Identify whether each patent covers a competitor product or process.
15. Identify gaps in technical coverage: technology areas in the product roadmap not covered by existing IP.
16. Identify redundant coverage: multiple patents covering the same or nearly the same technical concept.

### Phase 4: Business Alignment Assessment

17. Map each patent to current business units, products, or revenue streams.
18. Classify each patent by business relevance:
    - **Core:** Directly protects a significant commercial product or process.
    - **Strategic:** Does not directly protect current products but has licensing, blocking, or future value.
    - **Peripheral:** Tangentially related to current business; low commercial relevance.
    - **Non-core:** No connection to current or planned business activities.
19. Identify patents with licensing potential to third parties.
20. Identify patents that could be monetized through assertion or sale.
21. Identify patents that serve a purely defensive function (blocking competitors from patenting).

### Phase 5: Geographic Coverage Assessment

22. Map patent protection by jurisdiction for each technology area.
23. Identify whether key markets are covered: compare filing geography vs. commercial markets.
24. Identify under-protected markets: where the company sells but has no patent protection.
25. Identify over-protected markets: where the company has patents but no commercial presence or export concern.
26. Evaluate cost-benefit of maintaining protection in each jurisdiction for each asset.

### Phase 6: Portfolio Metrics & Benchmarking

27. Calculate portfolio KPIs:
    - Total active patent families and assets.
    - Average remaining patent life (weighted by business relevance).
    - Geographic breadth index (average number of jurisdictions per family).
    - Citation impact: average forward citations per patent.
    - Pending-to-granted ratio.
    - New filings per year (organic growth rate).
28. Benchmark against competitor portfolio metrics from competitive intelligence analysis.
29. Calculate pending portfolio value: estimate conversion rate and timeline for pending applications.

### Phase 7: Pruning & Maintenance Decisions

30. Identify candidates for abandonment or non-renewal:
    - Non-core patents with no licensing, blocking, or commercial value.
    - Patents with weak claims and high invalidity risk.
    - Patents in jurisdictions with no commercial relevance.
    - Redundant patents where one is sufficient to protect the position.
31. Calculate projected maintenance cost savings from proposed pruning.
32. Confirm proposed abandonments with business and technical stakeholders before execution.
33. Identify candidates for sale or licensing to generate revenue from non-core assets.

### Phase 8: Portfolio Strengthening Recommendations

34. Identify claim coverage gaps that should be addressed through continuation filings (broaden or alternate claim scope).
35. Identify technology gaps requiring new filings (from Phase 3 analysis).
36. Identify jurisdictions requiring additional filings for key products.
37. Recommend filing priorities based on business impact, competitive landscape, and budget.
38. Produce portfolio roadmap: 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year filing and maintenance plan.

### Phase 9: Reporting & Governance

39. Produce a portfolio scorecard with visual dashboards.
40. Produce a patent maintenance schedule and budget forecast.
41. Define portfolio review cadence (e.g., annual formal review, quarterly monitoring).
42. Document portfolio assessment methodology for audit purposes.

## 6. Technology Trends

**Purpose:** Identify emerging, growing, declining, and disruptive technology trends through analysis of patent data to inform R&D strategy, investment, and business planning.

### Phase 1: Domain & Time Horizon Definition

1. Define the technology domain(s) to be analyzed.
2. Define the historical time window (typically 10–20 years) and the trend forecast horizon (3–5 years forward).
3. Define granularity: analyze broad technology domains, specific sub-domains, or individual technical concepts.
4. Identify known major events in the domain that may have influenced patent filing (regulatory changes, product launches, platform shifts, standards adoption).

### Phase 2: Data Collection & Classification

5. Execute comprehensive patent searches across the domain for the full time window.
6. Classify patents by technology sub-domain using IPC/CPC codes and NLP-based text classification.
7. Assign each patent a technology maturity tag based on claim language: basic research, applied research, product development, manufacturing/process optimization.
8. Collect filing date, publication date, and grant date for each patent.
9. Collect citation counts (forward and backward) for each patent.
10. Collect assignee type: corporate, academic/research institute, government, individual.

### Phase 3: Trend Measurement & Visualization

11. Calculate annual patent filing counts by sub-domain — plot as time series.
12. Calculate CAGR of filings for each sub-domain over defined periods (e.g., 5-year, 10-year, full period).
13. Calculate 3-year rolling average to smooth noise in annual counts.
14. Identify inflection points: years where filing rates accelerated or decelerated significantly.
15. Identify peak-and-decline patterns: sub-domains with falling filings after a historical peak (technology maturation or obsolescence signal).
16. Identify recently emerging sub-domains: topics with near-zero filings before a specific year and growing counts since.
17. Apply S-curve modeling to patent filing data: classify sub-domains as:
    - **Emerging:** Early phase, exponential early growth, low total count.
    - **Growing:** Accelerating growth, increasing assignee diversity.
    - **Maturing:** Growth slowing, consolidating among fewer players.
    - **Declining:** Absolute or relative decrease in filings.
18. Compare filing trends to non-patent literature trends (academic paper counts, grant funding) to corroborate signals.

### Phase 4: Citation-Based Trend Identification

19. Identify "hot" patents: recently filed patents with unusually high and rapidly accumulating forward citations.
20. Identify citation bursts: sudden increase in citations to a group of older patents (indicates renewed interest or industry pivot).
21. Map citation clusters to identify emerging technology paradigms within the domain.
22. Identify highly-cited academic patents (often early indicators of commercial technology development).
23. Identify cross-domain citation flows: patents in one domain citing patents from another — signals technology convergence.

### Phase 5: Actor-Level Trend Analysis

24. Track changes in the mix of filers over time: are new entities entering? Are incumbents exiting?
25. Identify universities and research institutes filing in emerging areas — often earliest indicator of new technology directions.
26. Identify government-funded entities (e.g., national labs, DARPA-funded entities) filing in specific areas — signals strategic investment priorities.
27. Track inventor mobility: inventors from universities or incumbents starting new companies in emerging sub-domains.
28. Identify cross-industry entrants: companies from adjacent industries beginning to file in the domain.

### Phase 6: Technology Life Cycle Assessment

29. Apply Patent Life Cycle analysis (Strauss/Yoon) to each sub-domain:
    - Stage I: Invention — few inventors, few patents, basic research assignees.
    - Stage II: Growth — increasing inventors, corporate assignees dominant, applied research.
    - Stage III: Maturity — many inventors, dominant players consolidate, improvement patents.
    - Stage IV: Decline — fewer new filings, focus on manufacturing/optimization, NPE acquisitions.
30. Classify each sub-domain by life cycle stage.
31. Estimate transition timing: forecast when sub-domains are likely to shift to next stage.

### Phase 7: Synthesis & Strategic Implications

32. Rank sub-domains by strategic importance: growth rate × current size × business relevance.
33. Identify "watch list" sub-domains: emerging areas that may become commercially important in 3–5 years.
34. Identify "sunset" sub-domains: declining areas where R&D investment should be redirected.
35. Identify disruptive signals: new technical approaches that could displace dominant technologies.
36. Produce technology trend report: trend charts, life cycle maps, actor analysis, and strategic recommendations.
37. Set up automated monitoring for identified emerging sub-domains to track ongoing developments.

## 7. Market Analysis

**Purpose:** Correlate patent data with market dynamics to understand the commercial landscape, identify market opportunities, assess competitive positioning, and support product strategy.

### Phase 1: Market & Technology Boundary Alignment

1. Define the target market(s): product category, end-user segment, geography, and value chain position.
2. Map the technology domains that underpin the target market's key products and processes.
3. Align patent taxonomy with market segmentation (e.g., "automotive LiDAR" as both a market segment and a technology sub-domain).
4. Identify all relevant value chain participants: materials suppliers, component manufacturers, system integrators, OEMs, distributors, end users.

### Phase 2: Market Data Collection

5. Collect patent data for all identified technology domains (using Landscape Analysis methodology).
6. Collect market data from secondary sources: industry reports, trade association data, government statistics, financial filings.
7. Collect product launch and commercialization data for major players.
8. Collect M&A transaction data in the market segment.
9. Collect regulatory and standards data: relevant approvals, certifications, standards requirements.
10. Collect investment data: VC/PE investments in market participants, IPO activity.

### Phase 3: Market Structure Analysis from IP Data

11. Use patent assignee distribution to identify market concentration: Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) of patent ownership.
12. Identify IP-intensive vs. non-IP-intensive segments of the value chain.
13. Identify market participants that rely on trade secrets vs. patents (can be inferred from revenue/R&D ratio vs. patent counts).
14. Identify new entrants based on patent filing initiation dates (when a new entity started filing in the domain).
15. Identify potential new entrants from adjacent markets based on cross-domain patent filing analysis.

### Phase 4: Correlation of Patent & Market Activity

16. Correlate patent filing peaks with market growth phases: identify leading vs. lagging indicators.
17. Correlate geographic patent filing patterns with geographic market entry strategies.
18. Identify "patent-to-product" conversion rates: ratio of patent filings to actual product launches (varies by industry).
19. Identify IP white spaces that correlate with market demand gaps (unmet needs).
20. Identify IP concentration in market segments that are growing fastest — indicates where competitive pressure is highest.

### Phase 5: Competitive Market Positioning

21. Map each major patent holder to their commercial products and market share estimates.
22. Identify market leaders who have strong IP positions (sustainable competitive advantage).
23. Identify market leaders with weak IP positions (vulnerable to IP-based competition).
24. Identify patent-rich players with weak commercial position — potential licensing, partnership, or acquisition targets.
25. Identify patent-poor market entrants — assess their freedom to operate and exposure to IP risk.

### Phase 6: Regulatory & Standards IP Mapping

26. Identify regulatory requirements that mandate specific technologies — map to patents covering those technologies.
27. Identify standard-essential patents (SEPs) for mandatory industry standards in the market.
28. Assess FRAND licensing dynamics for SEPs — impact on cost structure for market participants.
29. Identify regulatory approval pathways that create or extinguish IP protection (e.g., FDA exclusivity periods, orphan drug designations, data exclusivity).

### Phase 7: Market Opportunity Identification

30. Identify high-growth market segments with sparse patent coverage — low IP barrier to entry.
31. Identify high-growth market segments with concentrated IP ownership — high barrier to entry, licensing required.
32. Identify expiring patent clusters in commercially active market segments — near-term genericization or open-source opportunities.
33. Identify geographic markets with strong commercial demand but weak patent coverage — expansion opportunities.

### Phase 8: Reporting

34. Produce market-IP correlation analysis: charts showing patent activity vs. market growth over time.
35. Produce competitive positioning map: IP strength vs. market share matrix for key players.
36. Produce market entry assessment: IP barriers by segment and recommended entry strategies.
37. Document data sources, dates, and confidence levels for all market data used.

## 8. Investment Analysis

**Purpose:** Evaluate the IP assets of a target company or portfolio for investment purposes — including M&A due diligence, venture capital diligence, private equity assessment, and IP acquisition transactions.

### Phase 1: Scope & Objective Definition

1. Define the type and purpose of the investment: M&A, venture investment, IP acquisition, joint venture, strategic partnership.
2. Define the target entity: single company, portfolio company, subsidiary, or specific IP assets.
3. Define the investment thesis: what role does IP play — core value driver, defensive moat, licensing revenue, or market access?
4. Define the geographic scope of the investment and relevant enforcement jurisdictions.
5. Identify the deal timeline and the IP due diligence deliverables required.

### Phase 2: Portfolio Inventory & Ownership Verification

6. Obtain or compile the complete IP schedule from target: patents, applications, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets, domain names, software licenses.
7. Verify ownership of each patent family through official assignment records (USPTO Assignment Database, national patent office records).
8. Identify any gaps in assignment chain: prior owners, missing assignments, employee-inventor assignments, contractor/consultant assignments.
9. Verify that all inventor assignments are in place (especially for university spin-outs or startups with academic founders).
10. Identify any IP created under government funding contracts — check for march-in rights (Bayh-Dole Act or equivalent).
11. Identify any IP created under collaboration agreements — check for co-ownership, license-back, or encumbrance provisions.

### Phase 3: Encumbrance & Lien Analysis

12. Search for recorded security interests (liens) against the IP portfolio in USPTO and relevant jurisdictions (UCC filings in the US).
13. Identify existing license agreements: exclusive, non-exclusive, field-of-use, sublicensable — assess impact on deal value and post-acquisition freedom.
14. Identify cross-license agreements: are there reciprocal licenses that come with obligations post-acquisition?
15. Identify any covenant-not-to-sue (CNS) agreements or patent pool commitments.
16. Identify FRAND or RAND declarations for SEPs — assess royalty rate implications.
17. Identify any IP subject to earn-out, royalty obligations, or purchase price adjustments.
18. Review material IP-related contracts for change-of-control clauses that may trigger license terminations or renegotiation.

### Phase 4: Portfolio Quality Assessment

19. Apply Portfolio Assessment methodology (Section 5) to the target's IP portfolio.
20. Conduct claim quality review on core and strategic patents.
21. Conduct validity risk assessment: prior art searches for key patents.
22. Identify any ongoing IPR, PGR, reexamination, opposition, or invalidation proceedings.
23. Identify any expired, abandoned, or lapsed patents that the target may be incorrectly representing as active.
24. Calculate weighted patent quality score for the portfolio.

### Phase 5: Coverage & Strategic Value Assessment

25. Map the target's patent portfolio to its current products, revenues, and pipeline.
26. Identify the "must-have" patents: those directly responsible for product exclusivity or competitive differentiation.
27. Assess the portfolio's coverage of future technology roadmap areas (for growth-stage companies).
28. Identify blocking patents against competitors: assess their enforcement value.
29. Identify licensing revenue potential: patents with broad claims applicable to third parties.
30. Assess portfolio defensibility: would the portfolio survive a concentrated validity challenge campaign by a well-funded competitor?

### Phase 6: IP Valuation (Quantitative)

31. Apply income approach: estimate net present value (NPV) of expected future cash flows attributable to the IP (licensing revenues, royalty savings, exclusivity premiums).
32. Apply market approach: identify comparable patent transactions or licensing rates in the technology domain.
33. Apply cost approach: estimate cost to recreate the IP portfolio (R&D investment, prosecution costs, time to develop equivalent innovations).
34. Apply relief-from-royalty method: estimate the royalty the business would pay to license the IP if it did not own it.
35. Conduct Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis: model value ranges across optimistic, base, and pessimistic scenarios.
36. Identify any "option value" embedded in pending applications or continuation opportunities.

### Phase 7: Risk Assessment

37. Identify patent expiry cliff: what percentage of portfolio value expires within 5 years?
38. Identify assertion risk: is the target a defendant in patent litigation? Are key patents being challenged?
39. Assess concentration risk: is the portfolio overly dependent on 1–3 patents for its core value?
40. Assess freedom-to-operate risk for the target's products against third-party patents.
41. Identify regulatory IP risks (e.g., compulsory licensing exposure in pharma or healthcare).
42. Assess international IP enforcement risk: are key patents enforceable in key markets?

### Phase 8: Recommendations & Reporting

43. Produce IP due diligence report:
    - Portfolio inventory and ownership summary.
    - Encumbrance and lien summary.
    - Quality tier breakdown.
    - Value-at-risk analysis.
    - Deal structure recommendations (representations, warranties, indemnities, escrow amounts).
44. Identify deal-breakers vs. deal-adjusters in IP findings.
45. Recommend IP-related representations and warranties for the transaction agreement.
46. Recommend any IP-specific conditions precedent to closing (e.g., cure assignment gaps, secure key employee IP agreements).

## 9. Litigation Analysis

**Purpose:** Analyze patent litigation data to assess assertion risk, litigation exposure, litigation history, and strategic posture for litigation decisions, portfolio management, and risk mitigation.

### Phase 1: Scope & Objective Definition

1. Define the purpose: assessing own litigation risk, evaluating a litigation target, supporting licensing negotiations, or conducting market-wide analysis.
2. Define the parties of interest: own company, specific competitors, NPEs/PAEs, or market-wide actors.
3. Define the technology domain and relevant time window.
4. Define jurisdictions: US federal courts, ITC, EPO Opposition Division, UKIPO, German Federal Patent Court, etc.

### Phase 2: Litigation Data Collection

5. Search US federal court dockets via PACER for patent cases involving parties of interest.
6. Search ITC Section 337 investigation records for relevant technology domains and parties.
7. Search state court records as applicable (rare for patent cases but relevant for contract/license disputes).
8. Search EPO opposition records for key European patents.
9. Search German Bundespatentgericht and Landgericht Düsseldorf/Mannheim records.
10. Search UK IPO and UKIPO High Court records.
11. Search databases aggregating patent litigation data: Docket Navigator, Lex Machina, Unified Patents, RPX, Innography Litigation, MaxVal.
12. Search PTAB for IPR, PGR, CBM, and ex parte reexamination proceedings.
13. Search ITC records for exclusion orders and consent orders.
14. Collect for each case: case number, filing date, parties (plaintiff/defendant), patents-in-suit, venue, claims asserted, status, outcome.

### Phase 3: Assertion Pattern Analysis

15. Identify all patent assertions by NPEs/PAEs in the relevant technology domain.
16. Identify all patent assertions by operating companies in the domain.
17. Map assertion patterns: which patents are being asserted most frequently?
18. Identify "serial asserters": entities with a pattern of repeated assertions in the domain.
19. Analyze assertion timing: do assertions correlate with competitor product launches, licensing negotiation breakdowns, or ownership changes?
20. Identify patents that have been asserted against multiple defendants (campaign litigation).
21. Analyze assertion geography: which venues are preferred? (E.g., Western District of Texas, District of Delaware historically popular in US.)

### Phase 4: Outcome Analysis

22. Compile win/loss rates for plaintiffs and defendants in the relevant domain and jurisdiction.
23. Analyze claim construction outcomes: frequency of favorable vs. unfavorable Markman rulings.
24. Analyze summary judgment rates: frequency of dispositive motions succeeding.
25. Analyze PTAB outcomes: institution rates, final written decision rates, claim cancellation rates for relevant technology.
26. Analyze ITC outcomes: institution rates, exclusion order grant rates.
27. Analyze settlement rates and timing: what percentage of cases settle? At what stage?
28. Identify any publicized settlement amounts or licensing rates as comparable data points.
29. Assess damages trends: jury award sizes, judge-imposed damages, ongoing royalty rates.
30. Assess enhanced damages (willfulness findings) frequency in the domain.

### Phase 5: Patent-Level Risk Assessment

31. For each patent of concern (own or third-party), assess litigation history:
    - Has this patent been asserted before? Against whom? With what outcome?
    - Has this patent survived IPR/reexamination? How many claims were cancelled?
    - Has this patent been subject to adverse claim construction?
    - Is this patent subject to terminal disclaimer linkage to a challenged patent?
32. Assess invalidity evidence: prior art identified during litigation or IPR proceedings against key patents.
33. Assess claim breadth sustainability: have courts narrowed broad claims in Markman proceedings?

### Phase 6: Counterparty Risk Assessment

34. Profile each potential litigation counterparty:
    - Operating company vs. NPE/PAE.
    - Litigation history: how many cases filed? Win rate? Settlement preferences?
    - Financial resources for sustained litigation.
    - Known outside counsel and litigation strategy patterns.
    - Portfolio size and quality.
35. Identify entities that have sold patents to NPEs — indicates potential indirect assertion risk.
36. Assess NPE ecosystem exposure: which NPE entities hold patents in the relevant domain? Who funds them?

### Phase 7: Own Portfolio Litigation Analysis

37. Identify own patents that have been asserted (offensively).
38. Identify own patents that have been challenged (defensively via IPR, PGR, opposition).
39. Assess litigation readiness of key portfolio assets:
    - Claim scope and definiteness.
    - Prior art exposure.
    - Availability of documentary evidence for damages (licensing comparables, commercial success).
40. Identify patents with prosecution history that creates vulnerability (e.g., estoppel from broad amendments).
41. Identify patents with chain-of-title issues that could be exploited in litigation.

### Phase 8: Risk Mitigation Strategy

42. Develop patent assertion risk response strategies:
    - IPR/PGR filing strategy against asserted third-party patents.
    - Defensive publication program to create prior art.
    - Portfolio expansion to create leverage for cross-licensing.
    - Defensive aggregation: joining RPX or similar programs.
    - License negotiation strategy using litigation risk data.
43. Develop own assertion strategy recommendations (if applicable):
    - Target identification and prioritization.
    - Venue selection rationale.
    - Claim set prioritization.
44. Produce litigation risk matrix: probability × severity for each identified risk.
45. Estimate litigation cost budget: average cost to defend in US district court, ITC, and PTAB by dispute type.

### Phase 9: Monitoring & Reporting

46. Set up litigation monitoring: automated alerts for new cases involving own company, competitors, or key patents.
47. Set up PTAB monitoring: alerts for new IPR/PGR petitions targeting own patents or key third-party patents.
48. Produce litigation landscape report: charts of case counts by year, party, venue, and outcome.
49. Produce risk-rated litigation watch list with recommended response actions.

## 10. Licensing Analysis

**Purpose:** Evaluate licensing opportunities, structure licensing programs, analyze in-licensing needs, and assess royalty rates for patent portfolios or individual assets.

### Phase 1: Objective & Scope Definition

1. Define the licensing objective: out-licensing (generating revenue), in-licensing (gaining freedom to operate or technology access), cross-licensing (mutual FTO), or licensing as part of M&A/partnership.
2. Define the target technology domain and specific patents or applications in scope.
3. Identify target licensees (out-licensing) or licensors (in-licensing).
4. Define field-of-use parameters: exclusive vs. non-exclusive, specific application fields, geographic scope.
5. Define the applicable time horizon: duration of license, step-down provisions, renewal options.

### Phase 2: Licensable Asset Identification & Assessment

6. Identify patents and applications suitable for licensing (using Portfolio Assessment to select core and strategic assets).
7. Assess claim quality and coverage for each licensable asset: what specific commercial acts do the claims cover?
8. Map each licensable patent to specific commercial products or processes of potential licensees.
9. Identify the "licensing footprint": which companies make, use, or sell products that may practice the claims.
10. Assess the validity of each key patent intended for licensing — strengthen weak patents or drop them from licensing program.

### Phase 3: Royalty Rate Benchmarking

11. Collect publicly available royalty rate comparables from litigation decisions, SEC filings, and licensing databases (RoyaltySource, ktMINE, Duff & Phelps IP Database, Royalty Stat).
12. Apply the 25% rule of thumb as a starting benchmark (25% of profit attributable to the IP) — note limitations and adjust based on facts.
13. Apply the Georgia-Pacific factors analysis for US-based licensing:
    - Factor 1: Royalties received for licensing similar patents.
    - Factor 2: Rates paid by licensee for other comparable patents.
    - Factor 3: Nature and scope of the license (exclusive, field, geography).
    - Factor 4: Licensor's established policy for licensing.
    - Factor 5: Commercial relationship between the parties.
    - Factors 6–8: Profitability of the product, contribution of the patented technology to commercial success.
    - Factors 9–12: Utility, advantages over prior art, commercial embodiments, portion of profit attributable.
    - Factor 13: Apportionment of profit between patented and unpatented features.
    - Factor 14: Expert opinions of qualified witnesses.
    - Factor 15: Hypothetical negotiation result (the central construct).
14. Apply FRAND analysis for SEPs: assess royalty stacking, smallest saleable patent-practicing unit (SSPPU), and proportionality.
15. Calculate royalty ranges: floor (cost-plus), midpoint (market comparable), ceiling (infringer's profit).

### Phase 4: Licensing Program Structure

16. Define license structure options for each asset or portfolio:
    - Per-unit royalty (running royalty): percentage of net selling price or fixed dollar per unit.
    - Lump-sum payment: one-time payment for defined period and scope.
    - Hybrid: upfront fee + running royalty.
    - Cross-license: mutual grant of rights with or without balancing payment.
    - Subscription: periodic fixed fee for defined usage.
17. Define license scope parameters:
    - Patent claims explicitly covered (by patent number and claim number).
    - Fields of use: specific products, industries, or applications.
    - Geographic territory: worldwide, by region, by country.
    - Exclusivity level: exclusive, co-exclusive, non-exclusive.
    - Sublicensing rights: permitted or prohibited.
    - Improvement licensing: grantback of improvements by licensee.
    - Most favored licensee (MFN) clauses: commitments to match lower rates.
18. Define reporting and audit provisions:
    - Royalty reporting frequency (quarterly, annually).
    - Licensee audit rights and audit procedures.
    - Interest on late payments.

### Phase 5: Licensee/Licensor Identification & Prioritization

19. Identify all entities in the licensing footprint.
20. Prioritize licensing targets by: revenue at risk (size of infringing activity), financial capacity to pay, relationship sensitivity, and likelihood of agreement.
21. For in-licensing: identify patent owners with assets needed for FTO, and assess their likely licensing posture.
22. Assess whether target licensees have cross-license leverage of their own — informs negotiating position.
23. Identify any government entities in the licensee population — special rules may apply (e.g., 28 USC § 1498 in the US).

### Phase 6: Licensing Negotiation Preparation

24. Prepare claim charts: map patent claims to licensee's specific products or processes (demonstrates infringement for out-licensing; demonstrates need for in-licensing).
25. Prepare validity and enforceability summary: proactively assess and address known weaknesses.
26. Prepare comparable license analysis: evidence supporting the proposed royalty rate.
27. Prepare economic impact analysis: quantify the licensee's commercial benefit from the technology.
28. Identify potential deal structures beyond pure licensing: equity stakes, research agreements, development collaborations.
29. Prepare BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement): litigation, partnership with alternative supplier, design-around.
30. Draft term sheet for initial negotiation.

### Phase 7: Post-License Management

31. Implement royalty collection and tracking system.
32. Conduct periodic royalty audits where contractually permitted.
33. Monitor licensee products for scope compliance (use-field adherence).
34. Monitor for new licensed patents maturing from pending applications (update license schedules).
35. Track patent expiry within licensed portfolio and adjust license terms accordingly.
36. Review and update licensing program as portfolio evolves.

### Phase 8: Reporting & Portfolio Licensing Strategy

37. Produce licensing program plan: target list, priority ranking, deal structure templates, royalty ranges.
38. Produce licensing revenue forecast: expected income by licensee and time period.
39. Produce licensing cost analysis: cost to administer program, litigation risk, opportunity cost.
40. Document licensing program for governance and audit purposes.

## 11. Valuation Analysis

**Purpose:** Determine the quantitative economic value of a patent, patent application, patent portfolio, or IP-intensive business for purposes of transaction, accounting, litigation, tax, licensing, or strategic planning.

### Phase 1: Valuation Purpose & Standard of Value Definition

1. Define the purpose of valuation: transaction (M&A, sale, license), accounting (ASC 805 purchase price allocation, ASC 350 impairment), litigation (reasonable royalty or lost profits damages), tax (transfer pricing, donation, estate), or strategic planning.
2. Define the standard of value: Fair Market Value (FMV), Fair Value (GAAP), Investment Value, Liquidation Value, or Strategic Value.
3. Define the effective date of valuation.
4. Define the premise of value: going concern (IP in use) vs. orderly liquidation vs. forced liquidation.
5. Identify the scope: individual patent, patent family, sub-portfolio, full portfolio, or enterprise value attributable to IP.

### Phase 2: Asset Identification & Characterization

6. Compile complete schedule of IP assets in scope (using Portfolio Assessment methodology).
7. Verify legal ownership and absence of encumbrances for each asset.
8. Confirm legal status: active, pending, expired, or abandoned.
9. Calculate remaining patent life for each asset, including any PTAs, SPCs, or regulatory extensions.
10. Document claim scope summary for key patents.
11. Characterize each patent by technology domain, product mapping, and commercial relevance.

### Phase 3: Economic Life & Remaining Useful Life Assessment

12. Assess the technical economic life: how long will the technology remain competitive and commercially relevant?
13. Assess the legal life: remaining patent term (not always equal to economic life).
14. Assess the functional life: could the technology be obsoleted by a disruptive substitute?
15. Determine remaining useful life (RUL) as the lesser of legal, technical, and functional life.
16. Apply a technology obsolescence factor where appropriate (especially for software, consumer electronics).

### Phase 4: Income Approach — Relief from Royalty Method

17. Identify the revenue base: the revenues generated by products or services that depend on the IP being valued.
18. Select a royalty rate: using benchmarking methodology from Licensing Analysis Phase 3.
19. Apply the royalty rate to the revenue base for each year of the remaining useful life.
20. Apply a tax rate to derive after-tax royalty savings.
21. Select an appropriate discount rate: reflects risk of the IP cash flows (typically WACC + IP risk premium, or based on cost of capital for the IP-intensive business).
22. Apply a discount factor to each year's royalty saving to derive present value.
23. Sum the present values to derive the total relief-from-royalty value.
24. Conduct sensitivity analysis: vary royalty rate (±25%, ±50%), revenue forecast (bear/base/bull), and discount rate.

### Phase 5: Income Approach — Incremental Income (Premium Profit) Method

25. Estimate revenues attributable to the IP vs. revenues that would be earned without the IP.
26. Estimate cost savings attributable to the IP (process patents, efficiency improvements).
27. Calculate incremental EBIT attributable to the IP each year over RUL.
28. Apply contributory asset charge (CAC) to strip out value attributable to other assets (assembled workforce, brand, working capital).
29. Apply appropriate tax rate to derive after-tax incremental income.
30. Discount at an appropriate rate and sum to derive present value.

### Phase 6: Income Approach — Excess Earnings Method

31. Identify total fair value of all tangible and identifiable intangible assets except the subject IP.
32. Apply a fair rate of return to each contributory asset to derive total required return on other assets.
33. Subtract total required return from the enterprise's total after-tax earnings to isolate excess earnings attributable to the IP.
34. Discount excess earnings over the IP's remaining useful life.
35. Apply this method primarily for primary intangible assets (e.g., core patent driving majority of business value).

### Phase 7: Market Approach

36. Identify comparable patent transactions: sales of similar patents in the same or adjacent technology domains.
37. Sources for comparables: IP transaction databases (ktMINE, Innography, Royalty Stat), public litigation settlements, SEC disclosures of IP transactions, patent auction results (ICAP, Ocean Tomo, AST).
38. Apply comparability adjustments for:
    - Remaining patent life at time of transaction.
    - Claim breadth and quality.
    - Technology domain relevance.
    - Market conditions at time of transaction vs. valuation date.
    - Portfolio size and composition (single patent vs. family vs. portfolio).
39. Derive an indication of value from market comparables.
40. Assess the reliability and applicability of each comparable; weight accordingly.

### Phase 8: Cost Approach

41. Estimate reproduction cost: direct cost to reproduce the exact IP (R&D labor, materials, testing, prosecution fees) at current prices.
42. Estimate replacement cost: cost to develop a functionally equivalent alternative.
43. Apply three forms of depreciation:
    - Physical deterioration: generally not applicable to patents (not physical assets).
    - Functional obsolescence: technological advances that reduce the utility of the IP.
    - Economic obsolescence: external market conditions reducing the earning capacity of the IP.
44. Subtract total depreciation from reproduction/replacement cost.
45. Note: cost approach is generally used as a floor value or sanity check, rarely as primary method for well-established IP.

### Phase 9: Synthesis & Reconciliation

46. Reconcile values derived from all applicable methods: income (relief-from-royalty, incremental income), market (comparables), and cost (floor check).
47. Weight each method based on the reliability of inputs, availability of data, and purpose of valuation.
48. Derive a point estimate or value range (range is often more defensible for complex portfolios).
49. Document all assumptions: revenue forecasts, royalty rates, growth rates, discount rates, life estimates, obsolescence factors.
50. Conduct scenario analysis: present optimistic, base case, and pessimistic values.

### Phase 10: Reporting & Compliance

51. Prepare a written valuation report meeting applicable professional standards:
    - **US (business valuation):** AICPA SSVS No. 1 or NACVA standards.
    - **Financial reporting:** ASC 820 (Fair Value), ASC 805 (Business Combinations), ASC 350 (Goodwill/Intangibles).
    - **Tax (US):** IRC § 482 Transfer Pricing regulations; IRS Revenue Procedure 59-60 (for going concern businesses).
    - **Litigation:** Daubert standard requirements for expert testimony reliability and methodology.
    - **International:** IVSC IVS 210 (Intangible Assets), RICS Red Book standards.
52. Include in the report:
    - Scope, purpose, and effective date.
    - Asset description and legal status summary.
    - Assumptions and limiting conditions.
    - Market and industry background.
    - Valuation methodology description.
    - Detailed calculations and exhibits.
    - Sensitivity and scenario analyses.
    - Conclusion of value with appropriate qualifications.
53. Retain supporting workpapers and data sources for audit and litigation defense purposes.
54. Define re-valuation triggers: M&A event, material patent grant or invalidation, significant market shift, annual impairment testing.
